(re)connect
Relationship intelligence for two.

(re)connect is a two-person relationship wellness platform that turns separate reflections from both partners into relationship-level insights, personalized prompts, and longitudinal progress reporting for couples seeking structured support between doing nothing and formal therapy.

The problem

Most relationship apps are built for one user, not two.

They reflect one person's perspective, not the relationship itself. Every product in the category processes one person's inputs and surfaces insight from that single vantage point. The relationship itself, the dynamic between two people, goes unmodeled.

That is not a relationship model. It is two individual wellness apps in the same household.

The solution

A platform that understands the relationship.

(re)connect is built for two. Each partner reflects separately. The platform holds both perspectives, then reveals what begins to emerge between them. Two partners. Two reflections. One relationship in view.

Why it works

Most relationship tools
only hear one side.

Most apps ask one person how the relationship is going. That answer is honest and incomplete.

(re)connect takes independent input from both partners and synthesizes the two streams into relationship-level intelligence neither could generate alone.

Two sides of the story.
A shared understanding of the relationship.

The synthesis is what sets it apart: more attuned over time, learning patterns that repeat, moments that matter, and where the relationship needs support.

Why users will love it

Built around dual input from both partners,
not one person's perspective.

Unlike apps that operate from one individual's perspective in isolation, (re)connect takes input from both people in the relationship and surfaces relationship-level synthesis to the couple, as a couple.

Why now

Three forces have converged
to make this buildable.

Consumers are paying for digital wellness.
The global mental health app market generated $7.48B in 2024 and is projected to reach $17.52B by 2030. Couples are already spending in this category. They are not getting a product built around the actual unit of the relationship.
AI can now synthesize structured inputs at scale.
Pattern recognition across two independent input streams, surfaced as synthesis that feels attuned to a specific couple, can now happen in software. Not as a replacement for therapy. As structured support between sessions.
The category is structurally underserved.
Most relationship products remain single-user at the core. No scaled product appears to have built paired longitudinal synthesis in this way. The architecture that mirrors how relationships actually work does not yet exist in the consumer market.
